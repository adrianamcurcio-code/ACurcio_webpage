ABOUT

Moving across countries taught me more about coordination than I ever expected.

Between visas, documentation, deadlines, translations, and navigating unfamiliar systems, I found myself naturally building processes, tracking details, and solving problems step by step.

That mindset has shaped so much of how I approach challenges. When something feels overwhelming, complex, or messy, my instinct is to break it down, create structure, and keep moving.

I’ve come to realize that this is simply how my brain works.

Looking back, I think this pattern has been present in almost everything I’ve done.

Professionally, I noticed the same thing in creative and digital work too — from freelance website projects to client collaboration, where I often found myself just as interested in what happened behind the scenes as the final outcome; the planning, the coordination, the communication, the problem-solving, and the quiet work of keeping things moving.

The Search Behind the Process

Before there were documents, timelines, and trackers, there was a much simpler problem: we didn't know who the Italian was. What started as a family story gradually turned into a years-long investigation across historical records, passenger lists, family trees, and more dead ends than I can count. We were trying to answer two simple questions: who was he, and where had he come from? The search for those answers ended up teaching me far more about research, persistence, and problem-solving than I ever expected.

Building the family tree

I started with fragments: names, dates, family stories, and a lot of missing information.

Over time, those fragments became a growing family tree that helped me connect generations and eventually identify the Italian ancestor we had been looking for.

But solving one mystery immediately revealed another: we still didn't know where he had come from.

Looking for a place

Finding the town became the next objective.

I explored passenger lists, ship manifests, immigration records, and historical archives, following every lead I could find.

Most searches led nowhere. A few helped narrow the possibilities.

The clue I almost missed

One of the most important breakthroughs came from an unexpected place.

While reviewing records, I noticed the same surnames appearing repeatedly as godparents across different branches of the family.

That observation led me to a hypothesis: if these families remained connected across generations in Argentina, perhaps they had also known each other in Italy.

So I started investigating them too.

Following that trail eventually led me to a small town in southern Italy: Sant'Arsenio.

After years of searching, connecting clues, and ruling out possibilities, the investigation finally had a destination.

For the first time, the story had a place.

Not the outcome I expected

Once I finally identified the town, I felt I was one step away from finding the records I needed.

At some point, online research stopped being enough.

So I booked a ticket and travelled to Italy alone to continue the search in person.

I arrived hoping to return home with the document in my hands.

That didn't happen.

Instead, I left with something I hadn't expected: conversations, local knowledge, and relationships that eventually helped me obtain the records I had been searching for.

Sometimes the solution comes from a different direction than the one you planned.

The process behind the process

The search eventually gave me the missing information I needed.

But finding the answer was only the beginning.

What came next was turning years of research into a structured process involving records, translations, corrections, apostilles, deadlines, and coordination across multiple countries.

That was when the challenge shifted from investigation to execution.

Building a system that gave me clarity
